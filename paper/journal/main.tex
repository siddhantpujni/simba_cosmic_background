What is a lightcone?

A lightcone is a geometric construct that represents the path of light travelling through spacetime from distant sources to an observer. In the context of galaxy surveys and cosmological simulations, a lightcone catalogue mimics what a real telescope would observe: galaxies at different distances are seen at different cosmic times, since light from more distant galaxies has taken longer to reach us. A galaxy observed at redshift $z = 2$, for example, is seen as it was approximately 10 billion years ago, when the universe was younger, denser, and galaxies were typically less massive and more actively star-forming.

Constructing a lightcone from a simulation involves stitching together multiple simulation snapshots, each representing the universe at a specific redshift. Galaxies are selected from each snapshot based on their spatial position within a defined survey area (in angular coordinates RA and DEC) and assigned an observed redshift based on their line-of-sight distance. This approach captures the evolution of the galaxy population across cosmic time within a single catalogue, rather than treating each snapshot as an independent, instantaneous view of the universe.

The resulting lightcone allows us to compute observable quantities such as the integrated cosmic background light. By summing the flux contributions from all galaxies in the lightcone---each appropriately dimmed according to its luminosity distance---we obtain a prediction for the total extragalactic background light (EBL) that would be measured by an observer. This is fundamentally different from summing galaxies at a single redshift, as it properly accounts for the fact that the galaxy population evolves with cosmic time and that more distant galaxies contribute less flux due to the inverse-square law and cosmological dimming.

Began by researching into the MBB and what the equation would look like. The MBB spectral energy distribution per unit wavelength is given by:

\begin{equation}
    S_\lambda(\lambda, T) = N \left( \frac{\lambda}{100\,\mu\mathrm{m}} \right)^\beta \frac{2 h c^2}{\lambda^5} \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{h c}{\lambda k_B T}\right) - 1},
    \label{eq:mbb}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $T$ is the dust temperature, $\beta$ is the dust emissivity index, $N$ is a normalisation constant, $h$ is Planck's constant, $c$ is the speed of light, and $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant.

Furthermore, to ensure the total far-infrared luminosity ($L_\mathrm{FIR}$) matches observations or model constraints, the MBB is normalised such that:

\begin{equation}
    \int S_\lambda(\lambda, T)\, d\lambda = L_\mathrm{FIR}
\end{equation}

This is achieved by computing the integral of the unnormalised SED and scaling the spectrum so its total energy output equals $L_\mathrm{FIR}$. This step was necessary to connect the theoretical SED shape to the observed or desired luminosity, ensuring physical consistency. This is the basic set-up for building the SED but right now the code computes for each individual galaxy and then sums all galaxies in a singular snapshot. However, similar to the optical SED, a lightcone will need to be used to generate the final SED to capture the effect over time/redshift.

Next step is working on radio emission part of the SED. Needed to do some literature review on how to actually approach to do this- Romeel gave me a paper \cite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021} and a rough method to follow but need to iron that out. Outline approach in more detail.

For radio emission:
Radio emission is primarily from AGN, many galaxies are heavily dominated by the central supermassive black hole (SMBH), particularly in active galaxies, radio-loud quasars, and elliptical galaxies. There is also contribution from star formation (SF) which is not as significantly but two sources contribute: non-thermal synchrotron from SNe/SNR and thermal from free-free radiation in HIII regions. \cite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021} models star formation radio emission using \cite{condonRadioEmissionNormal1992} we quote equations 10 and 11,

\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{non\text{-}thermal}} (\mathrm{W\,Hz^{-1}})
= 5.3 \times 10^{21}
\left(\frac{\nu}{\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-0.8}
\times \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot],
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{thermal}} (\mathrm{W\,Hz^{-1}})
= 5.5 \times 10^{20}
\left(\frac{\nu}{\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-0.1}
\times \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot],
\end{equation}

\noindent which are the contributions to total radio emission from thermal and non-thermal sources so total SF radio luminosity is $P_\nu = P_\mathrm{thermal} + P_\mathrm{non-thermal}$. $\mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot]$ is the rate of mass going into stars heavier than $5\,M_\odot$. We get this factor by computing the IMF mass fraction above $5\,M_\odot$ for the \cite{chabrierGalacticStellarSubstellar2003} IMF (learnt this in galaxies and cosmology literally today) which is:

\begin{equation}
    f_\mathrm{M\ge5} = \frac{\int_{5}^{100}m\xi(m)dm}{\int_{0.1}^{100}m\xi(m)dm}
    \label{eq:chabrierMassFraction}
\end{equation}

\noindent so $\mathrm{SFR[M\ge5M_\odot]=f_{M\ge5} \times \mathrm{SFR_total}}$ where I think we can simply get $\mathrm{SFR_total}$ from the \texttt{caesar} catalogues. We need to compute the mass fraction of newly formed stellar mass that lies above 5 $M_\odot$ so the IMF in mass form for $m>1M_\odot$ is a simple power law of the form,

\begin{equation}
    \xi(m) = Bm^{-2.3}
    \label{eq:chabrierPowerLaw}
\end{equation}
\noindent and for $m<1M_\odot$ we have the log normal distribution,

\begin{equation}
    \xi(m) = A \frac{1}{m}\exp{-\frac{(\log_{10}m - \log_{10}0.08)^2 }{2 \times 0.69^2}}
    \label{eq:chabrierLogNormalDistribuition}
\end{equation}

\noindent $A$ and $B$ are constants chosen to make the function continuous and normalised. Since the SFR is a mass rate we need the mass fraction which is \cref{eq:chabrierMassFraction} and then $\mathrm{SFR}_{>5}=f_{>5} \times \mathrm{SFR_total}$. Need to integrate \cref{eq:chabrierLogNormalDistribuition} numerically it has no nice analytic form, will just use \texttt{scipy.quad}

Secondly, uncertainties in the modelling of dust attenuation within the simulation may play a role. Dust affects optical emission by absorbing and scattering ultraviolet and optical photons, and any inaccuracies in the dust prescription—whether through overestimation or underestimation of attenuation—can significantly impact the resulting SED. While SIMBA includes prescriptions for dust production and destruction, these processes are inherently complex and may not fully capture the diversity of dust properties across different galaxy populations.


The predicted \textsc{simba} EBL, integrated from $z = 0 \rightarrow 7$, is presented in \cref{fig:finalEBL}. The redshift cutoff at $z = 7$ is justified by the negligible contribution of higher redshift sources to the total signal, as demonstrated by the light cone wedges in \cref{fig:lcWedge}. The three spectral components optical/NIR, far IR, and radio are shown separately alongside the curated observational EBL data from Driver et al., which is itself assembled from a range of independent surveys and measurement techniques. The heterogeneous origin of these data points means that systematic offsets between individual datasets are not unexpected, and should be borne in mind when interpreting residuals between the model and the observations. Overall, the predicted spectrum broadly reproduces the shape and amplitude of the observed EBL across the sampled wavelength range. The optical/NIR component is moderately under predicted, the far-IR peak is well-reproduced, and the radio is systematically over predicted. Jackknife uncertainties are shown as shaded bands but are generally small enough to be visually unresolvable on the full plot.

To isolate the contributions from distinct cosmic epochs, the EBL was computed separately in three redshift bins: $0 < z < 1$, $1 < z < 3$, and $3 < z < 7$, each constructed from an independent light cone with its own jackknife error estimate (\cref{fig:finalBinnedEBL}). The sum of the three bins recovers the full EBL of \cref{fig:finalEBL}, and the decreasing amplitude with increasing redshift bin confirms that the $z < 3$ universe accounts for the dominant share of the total EBL across all three spectral components. Contributions from $3 < z < 7$ are of order $10^{-2}\,\mathrm{nW\,m^{-2}\,sr^{-1}}$, further supporting the decision to truncate the integration at $z = 7$.

Several physically motivated trends are apparent in the binned decomposition. The optical/NIR emission is dominated by the $0 < z < 1$ bin, as expected: the apparent brightness of stellar populations falls with distance, so the local universe contributes disproportionately to the integrated starlight background. The far IR component peaks in the $1 < z < 3$ bin, coinciding with the epoch of peak cosmic star formation activity. Photons travelling from these distances traverse greater dust column densities, with UV and optical radiation progressively reprocessed into the far IR, whose emission is then further redshifted to longer observed wavelengths. The radio emission is likewise most prominent at low redshift, where radio loud AGN are both abundant and subject to minimal cosmological dimming.

The individual redshift bin estimates carry noticeably larger jackknife uncertainties than the full redshift prediction. This is a consequence of cosmic variance: each narrow bin subtends a smaller comoving volume, making the jackknife sub-volumes more sensitive to the stochastic distribution of bright sources. When integrated over the full redshift range, these fluctuations partially cancel across the larger ensemble of sub-volumes, reducing the relative variance in the total EBL estimate. This behaviour is consistent with the result that cosmic variance scales inversely with the square root of the survey volume \parencite{pass}.
