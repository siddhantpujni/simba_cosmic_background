\begin{abstract}
We present ground-based transit photometry of the hot Jupiter WASP-135b obtained using the Physics Innovations Robotic Astronomical Telescope Explorer (PIRATE). The images were calibrated using standard CCD reduction procedures and analysed via differential aperture photometry to extract its transit light curve. We also obtained and used standard star photometry to determine atmospheric extinction coefficients and calibrated magnitudes for WASP-135, and determine its classification to be a K2 star. This result lies within the 1$\sigma$ confidence region. We model the transit light curve in two ways: one with the impact parameter ($b$) sampled freely and one where $b$ is fixed to the literature value ($b$ = 0.80). For the fixed-$b$ model we obtain a planetary radius of \,$1.633^{+0.722}_{-0.350}\,R_\mathrm{J}$, a mass of $1.990^{+0.395}_{-0.236}\,M_\mathrm{J}$, a transit depth of $0.02175^{+0.00091}_{-0.00090}$, and a transit duration of $0.0662^{+0.0021}_{-0.0020}$\,days. The sampled-$b$ model yields a consistent mass of $1.973^{+0.391}_{-0.235}M_{\mathrm{Jup}}$, but a smaller radius of $1.452^{+0.636}_{-0.312}R_{\mathrm{Jup}}$. Overall, our results demonstrate that a 0.4-m class ground-based robotic telescope can recover physically plausible system parameters for a known exoplanet from a single observed transit epoch.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%In modern cosmology, two primary approaches are employed: hydrodynamical simulations and semi-analytic models (SAMs), which differ in both their computational complexity and the level of physical detail used to describe galaxy formation and evolution \parencite{neisteinHydrodynamicalSimulationsSemianalytic201}.

A fundamental question in cosmology is encapsulated by Olbers' paradox, which asks why the night sky appears dark despite the presence of an effectively infinite number of luminous sources. The resolution lies in the finite age of the Universe and the effects of cosmic expansion. Photons emitted by distant sources are redshifted as the Universe expands, shifting their energy to longer wavelengths and reducing their observed intensity. Consequently, the sky is not truly dark but instead filled with radiation across the entire electromagnetic spectrum. This diffuse radiation field is known as the extragalactic background light (EBL), representing the integrated emission from all sources over cosmic time. It is typically expressed as a specific intensity in units of $\mathrm{nW\ m^{-2}\ sr^{-1}}$ and has been measured across multiple wavelength regimes in several key studies \parencite{longairUniversalElectromagneticBackground1972,kashlinskyCosmicInfraredBackground2005,wessonOlberssParadoxSpectral1991,hauserCosmicInfraredBackground2001}.

A closely related quantity is the cosmic spectral energy distribution (CSED), which describes the total electromagnetic power produced per unit comoving volume as a function of wavelength at a given epoch. While the EBL represents the time integrated light from all redshifts, the CSED provides a snapshot of the energy output of the Universe at a specific redshift. In this sense, the EBL can be interpreted as the integral of the evolving CSED over cosmic time. Physically, the CSED arises from the combined spectral energy distributions (SEDs) of all galaxies within a representative cosmological volume, encoding information about the star formation history, dust content, and active galactic nucleus (AGN) activity.

Considerable observational effort has been devoted to measuring the CSED across different regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. Measurements in the ultraviolet \parencite{wilsonStarFormationHistory2002,budavariUltravioletLuminosityFunction2005,wyderUltravioletGalaxyLuminosity2005,robothamGALEXSDSSNUV2011}, optical \parencite{norberg2dFGalaxyRedshift2002,blantonGalaxyLuminosityFunction2003,montero-dortaSDSSDR6Luminosity2009}, and infrared \parencite{kochanekKBandGalaxyLuminosity2001,cole2dFGalaxyRedshift2001,saunders60mFarinfraredLuminosity1990,marchettiHerMESSubmillimetreLocal2016} regimes have been used to reconstruct the total energy output of galaxies. A complete CSED can be obtained either by combining measurements across these wavelength ranges or by analysing multiwavelength datasets covering large cosmological volumes \parencite{hillUgrizYJHKLuminosityDistributions2010,driverGalaxyMassAssembly2012,baesCosmicSpectralEnergy2019,andrewsModellingCosmicSpectral2018}. More recently, \cite{hillSpectrumUniverse2018} presented a comprehensive framework in which the electromagnetic spectrum is decomposed into its principal components (gamma-rays, X-rays, ultraviolet, optical, infrared, and radio), enabling a more detailed interpretation of the physical processes contributing to the total cosmic emission.

The shape and normalisation of the CSED, as well as its evolution with redshift, provide strong observational constraints on models of galaxy formation and evolution. In particular, different wavelength regimes trace distinct physical processes. Emission in the ultraviolet and optical bands is dominated by stellar populations, with young, massive stars contributing strongly at shorter wavelengths. In contrast, far infrared emission arises from interstellar dust grains that absorb ultraviolet and optical radiation and re-emit this energy thermally. The mid infrared regime is typically associated with emission from polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and more complex dust physics, while radio emission is produced through a combination of star formation processes (including synchrotron and free-free emission) and activity from radio loud AGN. Together, these components encode a comprehensive picture of the baryonic processes governing galaxy evolution.

In this work, the focus is placed on modelling the optical/near infrared, far infrared, and radio components of the CSED. The mid infrared regime is not included, as accurately modelling PAH emission requires detailed radiative transfer calculations using specialised tools such as \textsc{powderday}, which are beyond the scope of this study. The selected wavelength ranges nevertheless capture the dominant contributions from stellar emission, dust reprocessing, and radio activity, allowing for a meaningful reconstruction of the overall cosmic SED.

The primary aim of this project is to construct a prediction for the extragalactic background light from the \textsc{simba} cosmological hydrodynamical simulation and to compare this prediction with recent observational estimates, including forthcoming results from Driver et al. To achieve this, galaxy catalogues derived from \textsc{simba} are used to compute the contributions to the cosmic SED across different wavelength regimes. Lightcone techniques are employed to account for the evolution of the galaxy population with redshift and to produce a realistic observational analogue. The resulting SED is then analysed as a function of wavelength and redshift, and statistical uncertainties are estimated using jackknife resampling of the lightcone. By comparing the simulated EBL to observational constraints, this work aims to assess the ability of the SIMBA simulation to reproduce the global energy output of the universe and to identify potential discrepancies that may inform future improvements in galaxy formation models.

\section{Methodologies}

\subsection{The \texttt{SIMBA} Simulations}

Hydrodynamical simulations serve as theoretical laboratories for exploring the formation and evolution of structure in the Universe. By modelling the complex interplay between gravity, gas dynamics, star formation, and feedback processes, these simulations provide a controlled framework within which galaxy evolution can be studied. As observational capabilities through the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), \textit{Euclid}, and the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI), simulations play an increasingly important role in interpreting observations and refining our understanding of the underlying astrophysical processes. The \textsc{simba} simulations \parencite{daveSimbaCosmologicalSimulations2019} are hydrodynamical state-of-the-art simulations of galaxy formation, built on \textsc{gizmo} \parencite{hopkinsNewClassAccurate2015} using its meshless finite mass (MFM) method. Building on the previous generation \textsc{mufasa} simulations with improvements to the subgrid prescriptions for both star formation and active galactic nucleus (AGN) feedback via modified code from \textsc{gadget-3} \parencite{springelCosmologicalSimulationCode2005} it evolves dark matter and baryonic elements together including gravity and pressure forces.

\textsc{simba} approaches star formation based on molecular gas fraction following the $H_2$ Schmidt-Kennicutt relation with a fixed star formation efficiency $\epsilon_\star=0.02$ \parencite{robertc.kennicuttGlobalSchmidtLaw1998}. The star formation rate (SFR) is based on the $H2$ fraction which is computed via the \cite{krumholzCOMPARISONMETHODSDETERMINING2011} model. Stellar feedback is present via metal loaded kinetic winds that enrich the ISM for future star formation. Both torque limited \parencite{angles-alcazarGravitationalTorquedrivenBlack2017} and Bondi accretion \parencite{bondiSphericallySymmetricalAccretion1952,bondiMechanismAccretionStars1944} are used to model super massive black hole (SMBH) growth based on a cold accretion mode or a hot halo mode respectively. The resulting energetic feedback is modelled kinetically depending on the Eddington ratio which serves to quench galaxies along with X-ray energy feedback based on \cite{choiRADIATIVEMOMENTUMBASEDMECHANICAL2012}.

Finally, \textsc{simba} uses a self-consistent dust framework evolving directly from initial conditions, which is responsible for production, growth, and destruction \parencite{liDusttoGasDusttoMetalsRatio2019}. All dust grains are assumed to have the same constant size of $0.1 \mu m$ and have their motions coupled to gas flows. MORE DUST

The primary \textsc{simba} run is used for this work containing $1024^3$ dark matter and $1024^3$ baryonic particles in a comoving volume $100h^{-1}$Mpc in a redshift range $0<z<249$. The resulting mass evolution is $9.6 \times 10^7 M_\odot$ for dark matter particles and $1.82 \times 10^7 M_\odot$ for baryonic gas elements. The simulations follow a $\Lambda$CDM cosmology consistent with  Planck16 \parencite{planckcollaborationPlanck2015Results2016}, and parameters $\Omega_m = 0.3$, $\Omega_\Lambda = 0.7$, , $\Omega_b = 0.048$, $H_0 = 68 \mathrm{kms^{-1}Mpc^{-1}}$, $\sigma_8 = 0.82$, and $n_s = 0.97$. Simulation runs output 151 snapshots from $z=20 \rightarrow 0$ which are processed into \textsc{caesar} catalogues which contain the necessary pre-computed properties for galaxies and halos.\textsc{caesar}\footnote{caesar.readthedocs.io/en/latest/index.html} is a python based \textsc{yt} extension for analysing outputs from cosmological simulations through an intuitive interface without the original simulation binary. It outputs a \textsc{hdf5} catalogue that contains the galaxy and halo properties used for the analysis in this work. One may also conduct photometry for galaxy spectra using \textsc{fsps}.

\subsection{The Optical/NIR Regime}

Using the light cone described in \cref{sec:lcConstruction} we estimate the optical/near-infrared (optical/NIR) cosmic background by integrating the observed flux from all simulated galaxies. Using effective wavelengths for discrete photometric bands in \textsc{fsps}, a sky averaged intensity $I_\nu$ is calculated as a function of observed wavelength. We plot two apparent magnitude variants: (i) including dust attenuation, and (ii) a no dust baseline comparison, both of which are computed via \textsc{caesar}. This was done to highlight the importance of dust consideration when plotting the optical regime and the impact Rayleigh scattering has on flux observations. 

The optical/NIR range itself is determined operationally by the set of available filters. Each filter is mapped to an effective wavelength $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}$ using the \textsc{fsps} database. This is required so that band integrated photometric points are on a common wavelength axis for unit conversion. 

After $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}$ were determined the next step was to convert the apparent magnitudes stored in the \textsc{caesar} catalogues into a flux density. For each galaxy in the light cone, for each filter, the apparent magnitude, $m$, is converted to a flux density in the AB system via:

\begin{equation}
    f_\nu({\mathrm Jy}) = 3631 \times 10^{-m/2.5}.
\end{equation}
\noindent Next, for every galaxy appearing in the light cone, their flux densities are summed in each filter to get an integrated flux density, $F_\nu$. This accounts for any luminosity distance or K-corrections as we are using apparent magnitudes. We then further convert this to a mean specific intensity by dividing $F_\nu$ by the light cone solid angle,

\begin{equation}
    \Omega\ ({\mathrm sr}) = A\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right)^2,
    \label{eq:solidAngle}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $A$ is sky area of the light cone defined earlier. This finally yields $I_\nu$ presented in units of $\mathrm{erg\ s^{-1}\ cm^{-2}\ Hz^{-1}}$ for each filter $\lambda_\mathrm{eff}$. Finally, $\nu I_\nu$ is calculate using $\nu =\frac{c}{\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ to show how much energy is contained within a given range of frequencies. \subsection{The Far Infrared Regime}

dust model uncertainty ($\beta$, a parameter)

The far infrared (far IR) cosmic background is estimated by integrating the redshifted thermal dust emission from simulated galaxies along the constructed light cone. 

Dust emission deviates from a perfect blackbody, being cooler and less efficient at shorter wavelengths, and so each galaxy's far IR SED is modelled as a single temperature modified blackbody (MBB), or greybody, in which the Planck function $B_\nu(T)$ is scaled by a frequency dependent emissivity power law. This formulation assumes the dust is optically thin ($\tau \ll 1$), meaning radiation passes through without significant absorption \parencite{hildebrandDeterminationCloudMasses1983}.

Following \cite{caseyFarinfraredSpectralEnergy2012}, but in wavelength space via $\lambda = c/\nu$ (which introduces a factor of $\nu^2$ from the Jacobian), the rest frame optically thin MBB is

\begin{equation}
    S(\lambda) = N \cdot \left(\frac{100\,\mu\mathrm{m}}{\lambda}\right)^{\!\beta} 
    \cdot \frac{2hc^2}{\lambda^5} \cdot \frac{1}{\exp\!\left(\dfrac{hc}{\lambda k_B T}
    \right) - 1},
    \label{eq:dustmbb}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $N$ is a normalisation constant, $\left(100\,\mu\mathrm{m}/\lambda\right)^\beta$ is the dust emissivity power law anchored at $100\,\mu\mathrm{m}$, and $\beta$ is the spectral emissivity index. The normalisation $N$ is set such that the integral of $S(\lambda)$ over wavelength equals the galaxy's bolometric far-IR luminosity $L_\mathrm{FIR}$ (the total energy absorbed by dust extinction) computed by \textsc{caesar}. This ensures that more luminous galaxies contribute proportionally more flux to the background, while $T$ and $\beta$ govern the spectral distribution of that luminosity \parencite{caseyFarinfraredSpectralEnergy2012}.

An equivalent dust temperature $T_\mathrm{eqv}$ is assigned to each galaxy using the empirical relation calibrated against FIRE simulations by \cite{liangDustTemperaturesHighredshift2019},

\begin{equation}
    \log\left(\frac{T_\text{eqv}}{25\,\text{K}}\right) = a_{\mathrm{dust}} + 
    b\log\!\left(\frac{\delta_\text{dzr}}{0.4}\right) + c\log(1+z),
    \label{eq:equivalentDustTemp}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $b$ and $c$ are the best fitting regression parameters from that work, derived for a fixed $\beta = 2$ and an optically thin MBB evaluated at $870\,\mu\mathrm{m}$. $T_\mathrm{eqv}$ is not a resolved temperature distribution but a single effective temperature that, when paired with $L_\mathrm{FIR}$, reproduces the galaxy's far IR emission. The dust-to-metal ratio $\delta_\mathrm{dzr}$ is computed per galaxy as

\begin{equation*}
    \delta_{\mathrm{dzr}} = \frac{M_\mathrm{dust}}{Z \cdot M_{\mathrm{gasj}}},
\end{equation*}

\noindent where $M_\mathrm{dust}$, $Z$, and $M_\mathrm{gas}$ are taken directly from 
the \textsc{caesar} catalogues \parencite{liDusttogasDusttometalRatio2019}. The 
overall temperature scaling parameter $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ is treated as a free parameter and determined by fitting the simulated SED to observational data. The far IR spectra are restricted to $50$--$3000\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ to exclude contamination from the optical/near-IR and radio regimes. Model predictions are interpolated onto the observed wavelength grid and $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ is determined by minimising the $\chi^2$ statistic \parencite{jeyakumarContinuousOptimizationCurrent2006},

\begin{equation}
    \chi^2(a_{\mathrm{dust}})=\sum_i \left[\frac{M_i(a_{\mathrm{dust}})-D_i}
    {\sigma_i}\right]^2,
    \label{eq:continiousObjectiveFunction}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $D_i$ and $\sigma_i$ are the observed value and uncertainty at wavelength $\lambda_i$, and $M_i(a_{\mathrm{dust}})$ is the model prediction. Since $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ is sampled on a discrete grid, a secondary interpolation is performed along $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ to allow a bounded one dimensional minimiser to locate the best fitting value continuously. Goodness of fit is quantified via a reduced $\chi^2$. 

We present diagnostic plots of the temperature from the fitting of $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ in \cref{fig:tempDiagnostic}to highlight the spread of different possible temperature that the different $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ values give. We plot the best fitting value with 50$\sigma$ either side to show a reasonable spread otherwise you can not really see anything. The observational EBL data is also plotted to show the differences in the fits.

A logarithmic wavelength grid is adopted throughout to avoid undersampling at long wavelengths and oversampling at short wavelengths, appropriate for the broad spectral extent of dust emission.

To account for redshift, the normalised SED is evaluated at the rest frame wavelength $\lambda_\mathrm{rest} = \lambda_\mathrm{obs}/(1+z)$, and the observed flux density is

\begin{equation}
    f_\lambda(\lambda_{\mathrm{obs}}) =
    \frac{L_\lambda(\lambda_{\mathrm{rest}})}{4\pi d_L(z)^2 \cdot (1+z)},
    \label{eq:luminosityDistanceRelation}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $d_L(z)$ is the luminosity distance and the factor of $(1+z)$ accounts for the stretching of wavelength bins between the rest and observed frames \parencite{hoggDistanceMeasuresCosmology2000,gabrielliStatisticalPhysicsCosmic2005}. The sky-averaged intensity is then obtained by dividing the total flux by the light cone solid angle, as defined in \cref{eq:solidAngle}.

Synchrotron radiation is intense, polarized electromagnetic radiation emitted when charged particles (usually electrons) moving at relativistic speeds are accelerated along a curved path by magnetic fields

For the radio emission background we consider a broad interval from 10 MHz to 100 GHz to account for contributions from a range of different processes. This includes low frequency synchrotron dominated emission, the 1.4 GHz point often quoted in literature for radio SFR and AGN contributions, and the high frequency end where thermal/free-free contributions become important. The frequency grid is again log scale for even sampling. The radio emission is hence considered firstly from contributions by star formation and secondly from AGN radio luminosity.

Radio observations trace the SF within galaxies due to the synchrotron radiation emitted by relativistic electrons that arise from supernova and supernova remnants, as well as thermal bremsstrahlung (free-free radiation) from hot electrons in H II regions that is indicative of young hot stars \cite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021}. 

Radio observations from star formation in galaxies are decomposed into two components: 
non thermal synchrotron emission from cosmic ray electrons accelerated in supernova 
remnants, and thermal bremsstrahlung (free-free) emission from ionised gas in HII regions \cite{condonRadioEmissionNormal1992}. The total radio luminosity is modelled following \cite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021} as $P_\nu = P_\mathrm{thermal} + P_\mathrm{non-thermal}$, where the two components are

\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{non\text{-}thermal}} (\mathrm{W\,Hz^{-1}})
= 5.3 \times 10^{21}
\left(\frac{\nu}{\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-0.8} 
\times \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot],
\label{eq:nonThermalRadio}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{thermal}} (\mathrm{W\,Hz^{-1}})
= 5.5 \times 10^{20}
\left(\frac{\nu}{\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-0.1} 
\times \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot].
\label{eq:thermalRadio}
\end{equation}

\noindent Both relations are expressed in terms of $\mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot]$, 
which is the rate of mass going into stars more massive than $5\,M_\odot$, since only these 
stars produce the supernovae and ionising photons that drive the radio emission. This is obtained from the total SFR stored by \textsc{caesar} via

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot] = f_{M \geq 5} \times \mathrm{SFR_{total}},
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathrm{SFR_{total}}$ is taken directly from the \texttt{caesar} catalogues and $f_{M \geq 5}$ is the initial mass function (IMF) mass fraction above $5\,M_\odot$,

\begin{equation}
    f_{M \geq 5} = \frac{\int_{5}^{100}m\,\xi(m)\,\mathrm{d}m}{\int_{0.1}^{100}m\,\xi(m)\,\mathrm{d}m}.
    \label{eq:chabrierMassFraction}
\end{equation}

\noindent $\xi(m)$ is the \cite{chabrierGalacticStellarSubstellar2003} IMF, 
which takes the form of a power law above $1\,M_\odot$,

\begin{equation}
    \xi(m) = B\,m^{-2.3}, \quad m > 1\,M_\odot,
    \label{eq:chabrierPowerLaw}
\end{equation}

\noindent and a log-normal distribution below $1\,M_\odot$,

\begin{equation}
    \xi(m) = \frac{A}{m}\exp{\left(-\frac{(\log_{10}m - \log_{10}0.08)^2}
    {2 \times 0.69^2}\right)}, \quad m \leq 1\,M_\odot.
    \label{eq:chabrierLogNormal}
\end{equation}

\noindent The constants $A$ and $B$ are chosen to ensure continuity at $1\,M_\odot$ and normalisation over the full mass range. Since the log-normal form has no analytic integral, the denominator of \cref{eq:chabrierMassFraction} is evaluated numerically. The dependence on high mass stars of the radio luminosity calibration stems from their ionising photon production which drives bremsstrahlung, and they typically end their lives as core collapse supernovae which produce the synchrotron radiation observed \cite{shenoyWhatFactorsShape2026,bietenholzRadioLuminosityrisetimeFunction2021}.

While star formation can dominate the radio output of typical main sequence galaxies, AGN can dominate the radio emission for a subset of objects (especially at the bright end and for radio-loud systems) \parencite{pass}.

The AGN contribution to the radio background is computed from the black hole accretion rates of individual galaxies in the \textsc{simba} simulation. For each galaxy, the bolometric radio power is derived from the black hole accretion rate as presented in \cite{kordingMeasuringAccretionRate2007},

\begin{equation}
    \frac{P_\mathrm{Rad}}{10^{30}\mathrm{erg}\text{ }\mathrm{s^{-1}}} = \left(\frac{\dot{M}_{BH}}{4\times10^{17}\mathrm{g}\text{ }\mathrm{s^{-1}}}\right)^\frac{17}{12},
    \label{agnRadioEmission}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\dot{M}_{\mathrm{BH}}$ is the accretion rate of the most massive black hole in each galaxy as catalogued by \textsc{caesar}. Since the bolometric radio power represents the monochromatic luminosity $P_{\mathrm{Rad}} \sim \nu P_\nu$ evaluated at 1.4 GHz, the spectral luminosity is obtained by normalising as $P_\nu(1.4\;\mathrm{GHz}) = P_{\mathrm{Rad}} / \nu_{\mathrm{ref}}$, where $\nu_{\mathrm{ref}} = 1.4\;\mathrm{GHz}$. To evaluate the AGN radio emission across a frequency range (as was done for star formation), we assume a power-law spectral energy distribution of the form

\begin{equation}
    P_\nu(\nu) = P_\nu(1.4\;\mathrm{GHz})\,(\nu / 1.4\;\mathrm{GHz})^{-\alpha},
\end{equation}

\noindent adopting a spectral index of $\alpha = 0.7$ characteristic of radio loud AGN jets \parencite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021,angladaRadioJetsYoung2018}. The contribution of each galaxy to the observed specific intensity is then computed by evaluating $P_\nu$ at the rest frame frequency $\nu_{\mathrm{rest}} = \nu_{\mathrm{obs}}(1+z)$ and converting to observed flux density using \cref{eq:luminosityDistanceRelation} in frequency space. The AGN flux contributions from all galaxies in the lightcone are summed and divided by the survey solid angle in \cref{eq:solidAngle} to obtain the AGN component of the radio specific intensity as a function of observed frequency.

\subsection{Uncertainty Estimation}

One factor not considered so far are the implications of sources of uncertainty. The SED itself is derived from a finite sample, the construction of the light cone introduces a sampling variance from the inherent cosmic variance of the universe and hence a measure of statistical uncertainty is required. We implement the jackknife resampling method \parencite{bergerJackknifeVarianceEstimator2007} by dividing our light cone into 16 quadrants using RA/DEC quantile bins. For each realisation $k$ (0-15), we exclude the $k^{\mathrm{th}}$ quadrant and recompute the full SED background in each one to then calculate a mean and variance,

\begin{equation}
    \sigma^2 = \frac{N-1}{N} \sum (x_k-\bar{x})^2,
\end{equation}
