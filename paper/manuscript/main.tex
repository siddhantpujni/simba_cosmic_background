\begin{abstract}
We present ground-based transit photometry of the hot Jupiter WASP-135b obtained using the Physics Innovations Robotic Astronomical Telescope Explorer (PIRATE). The images were calibrated using standard CCD reduction procedures and analysed via differential aperture photometry to extract its transit light curve. We also obtained and used standard star photometry to determine atmospheric extinction coefficients and calibrated magnitudes for WASP-135, and determine its classification to be a K2 star. This result lies within the 1$\sigma$ confidence region. We model the transit light curve in two ways: one with the impact parameter ($b$) sampled freely and one where $b$ is fixed to the literature value ($b$ = 0.80). For the fixed-$b$ model we obtain a planetary radius of \,$1.633^{+0.722}_{-0.350}\,R_\mathrm{J}$, a mass of $1.990^{+0.395}_{-0.236}\,M_\mathrm{J}$, a transit depth of $0.02175^{+0.00091}_{-0.00090}$, and a transit duration of $0.0662^{+0.0021}_{-0.0020}$\,days. The sampled-$b$ model yields a consistent mass of $1.973^{+0.391}_{-0.235}M_{\mathrm{Jup}}$, but a smaller radius of $1.452^{+0.636}_{-0.312}R_{\mathrm{Jup}}$. Overall, our results demonstrate that a 0.4-m class ground-based robotic telescope can recover physically plausible system parameters for a known exoplanet from a single observed transit epoch.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%In modern cosmology, two primary approaches are employed: hydrodynamical simulations and semi-analytic models (SAMs), which differ in both their computational complexity and the level of physical detail used to describe galaxy formation and evolution \parencite{neisteinHydrodynamicalSimulationsSemianalytic201}.

\textit{Olbers' paradox} was a fundamental question in cosmology, it questioned why the night sky appears dark despite the presence of an effectively infinite number of luminous sources in an 'infinite' universe. The resolution lies in the finite age of the universe and the effects of cosmic expansion. Photons emitted by distant sources are redshifted as the universe expands, shifting their energy to longer wavelengths and reducing their observed intensity. Consequently, the sky is not truly dark but instead filled with radiation across the entire electromagnetic spectrum. This diffuse radiation field is known as the extragalactic background light (EBL), representing the integrated emission from all sources over cosmic time. It is typically expressed as a specific intensity in units of $\mathrm{nW\ m^{-2}\ sr^{-1}}$ and has been measured across multiple wavelength regimes in several key studies \parencite{longairUniversalElectromagneticBackground1972,kashlinskyCosmicInfraredBackground2005,wessonOlberssParadoxSpectral1991,hauserCosmicInfraredBackground2001,bernsteinOpticalExtragalacticBackground2007,vavrycukUniverseOpacityEBL2017}, however, the data sets have occasionaly had their reliability questioned \parencite{mattila1mmDiscontinuityExtragalactic2006}.

A closely related quantity is the cosmic spectral energy distribution (CSED), which describes the total electromagnetic power produced per unit comoving volume as a function of wavelength at a given epoch. While the EBL represents the time integrated light from all redshifts, the CSED provides a snapshot of the energy output of the universe at a specific redshift. In this sense, the EBL can be interpreted as the integral of the evolving CSED over cosmic time,

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{EBL} = \int \mathrm{CSED}(z)\frac{dt}{dz}dz.
\end{equation}

Physically, the CSED arises from the combined spectral energy distributions (SEDs) of all galaxies within a representative cosmological volume, encoding information about their star formation history, dust content, and active galactic nucleus (AGN) activity \parencite{andrewsModellingCosmicSpectral2018}.

One may measure the EBL by dividing it into a series of cosmic backgrounds decomposed into the electromagnetic spectrums principal components (gamma-rays, X-rays, ultraviolet, optical, infrared, and radio), enabling a more detailed interpretation of the physical processes contributing to the total cosmic emission \parencite{hillSpectrumUniverse2018}. Considerable observational effort has been devoted into measurements of the CSED to reconstruct the total energy output of galaxies; ultraviolet \parencite{wilsonStarFormationHistory2002,budavariUltravioletLuminosityFunction2005,wyderUltravioletGalaxyLuminosity2005,robothamGALEXSDSSNUV2011}, optical \parencite{norberg2dFGalaxyRedshift2002,blantonGalaxyLuminosityFunction2003,montero-dortaSDSSDR6Luminosity2009}, and infrared \parencite{kochanekKBandGalaxyLuminosity2001,cole2dFGalaxyRedshift2001,saunders60mFarinfraredLuminosity1990,marchettiHerMESSubmillimetreLocal2016}. However, this approach leads to inconsistent results due to variance in source selection, cosmic variance, and differences in approach \parencite{driverGalaxyMassAssembly2012,crossBivariateBrightnessFunction2002}. A complete CSED can be obtained either by combining measurements across these wavelength ranges or by analysing multiwavelength datasets covering large cosmological volumes \parencite{hillUgrizYJHKLuminosityDistributions2010,driverGalaxyMassAssembly2011,baesCosmicSpectralEnergy2019,andrewsModellingCosmicSpectral2018}. 

The shape and normalisation of the CSED, as well as its evolution with redshift, provide strong observational constraints on models of galaxy formation and evolution \parencite{dominguezExtragalacticBackgroundLight2011}. Furthermore, we may use the EBl to test for global star formation history \parencite{fardalEvolutionaryHistoryStars2007} as well. The EBL is expected to exhibit two dominant peaks. The first is in the optical and ultraviolet, which arises primarily from stellar populations within galaxies, particularly young massive stars that emit strongly at short wavelengths \cite{schlegelMapsDustInfrared1998}. A second peak in the far infrared results from interstellar dust grains absorbing ultraviolet and optical radiation and re-emitting this energy thermally \parencite{calzettiDustContentOpacity2000}. 

In particular, different wavelength regimes trace distinct physical processes. Emission in the ultraviolet and optical bands is dominated by stellar populations, with young, massive stars contributing strongly at shorter wavelengths. In contrast, far-infrared emission arises from interstellar dust grains that absorb ultraviolet and optical radiation and re-emit this energy thermally. The mid infrared regime is typically associated with emission from polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) which are carbon based nanoparticles reprocessing UV light and more complex dust physics, while radio emission is produced through a combination of star formation processes (including synchrotron and free-free emission) and activity from radio loud AGN. Together, these components encode a comprehensive picture of the baryonic processes governing galaxy evolution.

To further extract information from the EBL, it may be divided into pre- and post- decoupling phases. The well known cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation marks the end of the big bang and pre-decoupling phase. The post decoupling phase then marks everything that has happened since, encoding all of galaxy formation and evolution to date. The post-decoupling EBL is then traced by five photon pathways which are then reflected/found in the different electromagnetic spectrum slices the EBL may be presented in; (i) star-formation in the UV, optical, and radio \parencite{partridgeAreYoungGalaxies1967,tinsleyGalaxyCountsColorredshift1977}, (ii) accreting SMBH's in X-ray \parencite{silvaConnectingCosmicInfrared2004,ballantyneContributionActiveGalactic2007}, (iii) AGN Jets in Radio \parencite{khaireNewSynthesisModels2019}, (iv) dust subsequently heated by these processes in the Far IR \parencite{khaireNewSynthesisModels2019}, and (v) interaction of charged particles with either other particles (free-free) or magnetic fields (synchrotron) in Radio \parencite{marvilIntegratedRadioContinuum2015}  (need to expand and explain a little more here) all these photon pathways constrain information about those specific regions and tell us more about them as done in these papers: eofn give examples of using ebl for stuff

In this work focus is placed on modelling the optical/near infrared, far infrared, and radio components. The mid infrared regime is not included, as accurately modelling PAH emission requires detailed radiative transfer calculations using specialised tools such as \textsc{powderday}, which are beyond the scope of this study. The selected wavelength ranges nevertheless capture the dominant contributions from stellar emission, dust reprocessing, and radio activity, allowing for a meaningful reconstruction of the overall EBL.

The primary aim of this project is to construct a prediction for the EBL from the \textsc{simba} simulations and compare this prediction with recent observational estimates from Driver et al, an upcoming paper that combines and mixes data from a range of different sources and points to make an EBL. To achieve this, galaxy catalogues derived from \textsc{simba} are used to compute the contributions to the cosmic SED across different wavelength regimes. Light cone techniques are employed to account for the evolution of the galaxy population with redshift and to produce a realistic observational analogue. The resulting SED is then analysed as a function of wavelength and redshift, and statistical uncertainties are estimated using jackknife resampling of the light cone. By comparing the simulated EBL to observational constraints, this work aims to assess the ability of the SIMBA simulation to reproduce the global energy output of the universe and to identify potential discrepancies that may inform future improvements in galaxy formation models.

\section{Methodologies}

\subsection{The \texttt{SIMBA} Simulations}

Hydrodynamical simulations serve as theoretical laboratories for exploring the formation and evolution of structure in the Universe. By modelling the complex interplay between gravity, gas dynamics, star formation, and feedback processes, these simulations provide a controlled framework within which galaxy evolution can be studied. As observational capabilities through the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), \textit{Euclid}, and the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI), simulations play an increasingly important role in interpreting observations and refining our understanding of the underlying astrophysical processes. The \textsc{simba} simulations \parencite{daveSimbaCosmologicalSimulations2019} are hydrodynamical state-of-the-art simulations of galaxy formation, built on \textsc{gizmo} \parencite{hopkinsNewClassAccurate2015} using its meshless finite mass (MFM) method. Building on the previous generation \textsc{mufasa} simulations with improvements to the subgrid prescriptions for both star formation and active galactic nucleus (AGN) feedback via modified code from \textsc{gadget-3} \parencite{springelCosmologicalSimulationCode2005} it evolves dark matter and baryonic elements together including gravity and pressure forces.

\textsc{simba} approaches star formation based on molecular gas fraction following the $H_2$ Schmidt-Kennicutt relation with a fixed star formation efficiency $\epsilon_\star=0.02$ \parencite{robertc.kennicuttGlobalSchmidtLaw1998}. The star formation rate (SFR) is based on the $H2$ fraction which is computed via the \cite{krumholzCOMPARISONMETHODSDETERMINING2011} model. Stellar feedback is present via metal loaded kinetic winds that enrich the ISM for future star formation. Both torque limited \parencite{angles-alcazarGravitationalTorquedrivenBlack2017} and Bondi accretion \parencite{bondiSphericallySymmetricalAccretion1952,bondiMechanismAccretionStars1944} are used to model super massive black hole (SMBH) growth based on a cold accretion mode or a hot halo mode respectively. The resulting energetic feedback is modelled kinetically depending on the Eddington ratio which serves to quench galaxies along with X-ray energy feedback based on \cite{choiRADIATIVEMOMENTUMBASEDMECHANICAL2012}.

Finally, \textsc{simba} uses a self-consistent dust framework evolving directly from initial conditions, which is responsible for production, growth, and destruction \parencite{liDusttoGasDusttoMetalsRatio2019}. All dust grains are assumed to have the same constant size of $0.1 \mu m$ and have their motions coupled to gas flows. Dust production is informed by Type II supernovae (SNe) and Asymptotic Giant Branch stars, and further growth via condensation from metals, while its destruction can occur from sputtering, consumption by star formation, or SNe shocks.

The primary \textsc{simba} run is used for this work containing $1024^3$ dark matter and $1024^3$ baryonic particles in a comoving volume $100h^{-1}$Mpc in a redshift range $0<z<249$. The resulting mass evolution is $9.6 \times 10^7 M_\odot$ for dark matter particles and $1.82 \times 10^7 M_\odot$ for baryonic gas elements. The simulations follow a $\Lambda$CDM cosmology consistent with Planck16 \parencite{planckcollaborationPlanck2015Results2016}, and parameters $\Omega_m = 0.3$, $\Omega_\Lambda = 0.7$, , $\Omega_b = 0.048$, $H_0 = 68 \mathrm{kms^{-1}Mpc^{-1}}$, $\sigma_8 = 0.82$, and $n_s = 0.97$. Simulation runs output 151 snapshots from $z=20 \rightarrow 0$ which are processed into \textsc{caesar} catalogues which contain the necessary pre-computed properties for galaxies and halos.\textsc{ caesar}\footnote{caesar.readthedocs.io/en/latest/index.html} is a python based \textsc{yt} extension for analysing outputs from cosmological simulations through an intuitive interface without the original simulation binary. It outputs a \textsc{hdf5} catalogue that contains the galaxy and halo properties used for the analysis in this work. One may also conduct photometry for galaxy spectra using \textsc{fsps}.

\subsection{Light Cones}
\label{subsec:lcConstruction}

A light cone is a geometric construct typically seen in special relativity that represents the path of light travelling through spacetime from distant sources to an observer. In our context of galaxy surveys and cosmological simulations, a light cone catalogue allows us to mimic what a real telescope would observe: galaxies at different distances are seen at different cosmic times, so we account for the fact that the galaxy population evolves with cosmic time and that more distant galaxies contribute less flux due to the inverse square law and cosmological dimming. Constructing a light cone with the \textsc{simba} simulations involves stitching together snapshots, each representing the universe at a specific redshift, we adopt light cone generation for \textsc{simba} as discussed in \cite{lovellReproducingSubmillimetreGalaxy2021}\footnote{github.com/christopherlovell/simba\_lightcone\_generation}. The light cone was only considered with a 0.5 $ \mathrm{deg}^2$ sky area and up till $z=7$ as snapshots beyond this either did not contain any halo data or had inconsequential contributions to the overall EBL (need to explain sky area choice and not sure where to include redshift information).

The resulting light cone allows us to compute the necessary observable quantities such as the integrated cosmic background spectrum to form the respective SED for each electromagnetic spectrum component. By summing the flux contributions from all galaxies in the light cone, each appropriately dimmed according to its luminosity distance, we obtain a prediction for the total EBL that would be measured by an observer.

\subsection{The Optical/NIR Regime}

Using the light cone construction described above we estimate the optical/near-infrared (optical/NIR) cosmic background by integrating the observed flux from all simulated galaxies. Using effective wavelengths for discrete photometric bands in \textsc{fsps}, a sky averaged intensity $I_\nu$ is calculated as a function of observed wavelength. We plot the apparent magnitude including dust attenuation computed via \textsc{caesar}. We use the apparent magnitude as this is the intrinsic luminosity of the object and already accounts for any luminosity distance corrections needed for  

The optical/NIR range itself is determined operationally by the set of available filters. Each filter is mapped to an effective wavelength $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}$ using the \textsc{fsps} database. This is required so that band integrated photometric points are on a common wavelength axis for unit conversion. 

After $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}$ were determined the next step was to convert the apparent magnitudes stored in the \textsc{caesar} catalogues into a flux density. For each galaxy in the light cone, for each filter, the apparent magnitude, $m$, is converted to a flux density in the AB system via:

\begin{equation}
    f_\nu({\mathrm Jy}) = 3631 \times 10^{-m/2.5}.
\end{equation}
\noindent Next, for every galaxy appearing in the light cone, their flux densities are summed in each filter to get an integrated flux density, $F_\nu$. This accounts for any luminosity distance or K-corrections as we are using apparent magnitudes. We then further convert this to a mean specific intensity by dividing $F_\nu$ by the light cone solid angle,

\begin{equation}
    \Omega\ ({\mathrm sr}) = A\left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right)^2,
    \label{eq:solidAngle}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $A$ is sky area of the light cone defined earlier. This yields $I_\nu$ presented in units of $\mathrm{erg\ s^{-1}\ cm^{-2}\ Hz^{-1}}$ for each filter $\lambda_\mathrm{eff}$. Finally, $\nu I_\nu$ is calculated using $\nu =\frac{c}{\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ to show how much energy is contained within a given range of frequencies. It is particularly useful because it allows identification of which frequency band dominates the total energy emission (the peak of the plot represents the maximum power).

\subsection{The Far Infrared Regime}

The far infrared (far IR) cosmic background is estimated by integrating the redshifted thermal dust emission from simulated galaxies along the constructed light cone. 

Dust emission deviates from a perfect blackbody, being cooler and less efficient at shorter wavelengths, and so each galaxy's far IR SED is modelled as a single temperature modified blackbody (MBB), or greybody, in which the Planck function $B_\nu(T)$ is scaled by a frequency dependent emissivity power law. This formulation assumes the dust is optically thin ($\tau \ll 1$), meaning radiation passes through without significant absorption \parencite{hildebrandDeterminationCloudMasses1983}.

Following \cite{caseyFarinfraredSpectralEnergy2012}, but in wavelength space via $\lambda = c/\nu$ (which introduces a factor of $\nu^2$ from the Jacobian), the rest frame optically thin MBB is

\begin{equation}
    S(\lambda) = N \cdot \left(\frac{100\,\mu\mathrm{m}}{\lambda}\right)^{\!\beta} 
    \cdot \frac{2hc^2}{\lambda^5} \cdot \frac{1}{\exp\!\left(\dfrac{hc}{\lambda k_B T}
    \right) - 1},
    \label{eq:dustmbb}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $N$ is a normalisation constant, $\left(100\,\mu\mathrm{m}/\lambda\right)^\beta$ is the dust emissivity power law anchored at $100\,\mu\mathrm{m}$, and $\beta$ is the spectral emissivity index (how efficiently a surface emits radiation at a specific wavelength). The normalisation $N$ is set such that the integral of $S(\lambda)$ over wavelength equals the galaxy's bolometric far-IR luminosity $L_\mathrm{FIR}$ (the total energy absorbed by dust extinction) computed by \textsc{caesar}. This ensures that more luminous galaxies contribute proportionally more flux to the background, while $T$ and $\beta$ govern the spectral distribution of that luminosity \parencite{caseyFarinfraredSpectralEnergy2012}.

An equivalent dust temperature $T_\mathrm{eqv}$ is assigned to each galaxy using the empirical relation calibrated against FIRE simulations by \cite{liangDustTemperaturesHighredshift2019},

\begin{equation}
    \log\left(\frac{T_\text{eqv}}{25\,\text{K}}\right) = a_{\mathrm{dust}} + 
    b\log\!\left(\frac{\delta_\text{dzr}}{0.4}\right) + c\log(1+z),
    \label{eq:equivalentDustTemp}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $b$ and $c$ are the best fitting regression parameters from that work, derived for a fixed $\beta = 2$ and an optically thin MBB evaluated at $870\,\mu\mathrm{m}$. $T_\mathrm{eqv}$ is not a resolved temperature distribution but a single effective temperature that, when paired with $L_\mathrm{FIR}$, reproduces the galaxy's far IR emission. The dust-to-metal ratio $\delta_\mathrm{dzr}$ is computed per galaxy as

\begin{equation*}
    \delta_{\mathrm{dzr}} = \frac{M_\mathrm{dust}}{Z \cdot M_{\mathrm{gasj}}},
\end{equation*}

\noindent where $M_\mathrm{dust}$, $Z$, and $M_\mathrm{gas}$ are taken directly from 
the \textsc{caesar} catalogues \parencite{liDusttoGasDusttoMetalsRatio2019}. The 
overall temperature scaling parameter $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ is treated as a free parameter and determined by fitting the simulated SED to observational data. The far IR spectra are restricted to $50$--$3000\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ to exclude contamination from the optical/near-IR and radio regimes. Model predictions are interpolated onto the observed wavelength grid and $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ is determined by minimising the $\chi^2$ statistic \parencite{jeyakumarContinuousOptimizationCurrent2006},

\begin{equation}
    \chi^2(a_{\mathrm{dust}})=\sum_i \left[\frac{M_i(a_{\mathrm{dust}})-D_i}
    {\sigma_i}\right]^2,
    \label{eq:continiousObjectiveFunction}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $D_i$ and $\sigma_i$ are the observed value and uncertainty at wavelength $\lambda_i$, and $M_i(a_{\mathrm{dust}})$ is the model prediction. Since $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ is sampled on a discrete grid, a secondary interpolation is performed along $a_{\mathrm{dust}}$ to allow a bounded one dimensional minimiser to locate the best fitting value continuously. Goodness of fit is quantified via a reduced $\chi^2$.

To account for redshift, the normalised SED is evaluated at the rest frame wavelength $\lambda_\mathrm{rest} = \lambda_\mathrm{obs}/(1+z)$, and the observed flux density is

\begin{equation}
    f_\lambda(\lambda_{\mathrm{obs}}) =
    \frac{L_\lambda(\lambda_{\mathrm{rest}})}{4\pi d_L(z)^2 \cdot (1+z)},
    \label{eq:luminosityDistanceRelation}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $d_L(z)$ is the luminosity distance and the factor of $(1+z)$ accounts for the stretching of wavelength bins between the rest and observed frames \parencite{hoggDistanceMeasuresCosmology2000,gabrielliStatisticalPhysicsCosmic2005}. The sky averaged intensity is then obtained by dividing the total flux by the light cone solid angle, as defined in \cref{eq:solidAngle}. A logarithmic wavelength grid is adopted throughout to avoid undersampling at long wavelengths and oversampling at short wavelengths.

\subsection{The Radio Regime}

For the radio emission background we consider a broad interval from 10 MHz to 100 GHz to account for contributions from a range of different processes. This includes low frequency synchrotron dominated emission, the 1.4 GHz point often quoted in literature for radio SFR and AGN contributions, and the high frequency end where thermal/free-free contributions become important. The frequency grid is again log scale for even sampling. The radio emission is hence considered firstly from contributions by star formation and secondly from AGN radio luminosity. dominated by AGN over SF and stuff CITE

Radio observations trace the star formation within galaxies due to the synchrotron radiation emitted by relativistic electrons accelerated by magnetic fields that arise from supernova and supernova remnants, as well as thermal bremsstrahlung (free-free radiation) from hot electrons in HII regions that is indicative of young hot stars \cite{kopsacheiliNewXraySupernova2025}. 

These observations are decomposed into two components: non thermal synchrotron emission from cosmic ray electrons accelerated in supernova remnants, and the thermal bremsstrahlung (free-free) emission from ionised gas in HII regions \cite{condonRadioEmissionNormal1992}. The total radio luminosity is modelled following \cite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021} as $P_\nu = P_\mathrm{non-thermal} + P_\mathrm{thermal}$, where

\begin{multline}
    P_{\mathrm{non\text{-}thermal}} (\mathrm{W\,Hz^{-1}})
= 5.3 \times 10^{21}
\left(\frac{\nu}{\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-0.8} \\
\times \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot],
\label{eq:nonThermalRadio}
\end{multline}

\begin{multline}
P_{\mathrm{thermal}} (\mathrm{W\,Hz^{-1}})
= 5.5 \times 10^{20}
\left(\frac{\nu}{\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-0.1} \\
\times \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot].
\label{eq:thermalRadio}
\end{multline}

\noindent Both relations are expressed in terms of $\mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot]$, which is the rate of mass going into stars more massive than $5\,M_\odot$, since only these stars produce the supernovae and ionising photons that drive the radio emission. This is obtained from the total SFR by

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{SFR}[M \geq 5\,M_\odot] = f_{M \geq 5} \times \mathrm{SFR_{total}},
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathrm{SFR_{total}}$ is taken directly from the \texttt{caesar} catalogues and $f_{M \geq 5}$ is the initial mass function (IMF) mass fraction above $5\,M_\odot$,

\begin{equation}
    f_{M \geq 5} = \frac{\int_{5}^{100}m\,\xi(m)\,\mathrm{d}m}{\int_{0.1}^{100}m\,\xi(m)\,\mathrm{d}m}.
    \label{eq:chabrierMassFraction}
\end{equation}

\noindent $\xi(m)$ is the \cite{chabrierGalacticStellarSubstellar2003} IMF, 
which takes the form of a power law above $1\,M_\odot$,

\begin{equation}
    \xi(m) = B\,m^{-2.3}, \quad m > 1\,M_\odot,
    \label{eq:chabrierPowerLaw}
\end{equation}

\noindent and a log-normal distribution below $1\,M_\odot$,

\begin{equation}
    \xi(m) = \frac{A}{m}\exp{\left(-\frac{(\log_{10}m - \log_{10}0.08)^2}
    {2 \times 0.69^2}\right)}, \quad m \leq 1\,M_\odot.
    \label{eq:chabrierLogNormal}
\end{equation}

\noindent The constants $A$ and $B$ are chosen to ensure continuity at $1\,M_\odot$ and normalisation over the full mass range. Since the log-normal form has no analytic integral, the denominator of \cref{eq:chabrierMassFraction} is evaluated numerically. The dependence on high mass stars of the radio luminosity calibration stems from their ionising photon production which drives bremsstrahlung, and they typically end their lives as core collapse supernovae which produce the synchrotron radiation observed \cite{shenoyWhatFactorsShape2026,bietenholzRadioLuminosityrisetimeFunction2021}.

While star formation can dominate the radio output of typical main sequence galaxies, AGN dominate the radio emission for a subset of objects (especially at the bright end and for radio-loud systems) \parencite{pass}. The AGN contribution to the radio background is computed from the black hole accretion rates of individual galaxies in the \textsc{simba} simulation. For each galaxy, the bolometric radio power is derived from the black hole accretion rate as presented in \cite{kordingMeasuringAccretionRate2007},

\begin{equation}
    \frac{P_\mathrm{Rad}}{10^{30}\mathrm{erg}\text{ }\mathrm{s^{-1}}} = \left(\frac{\dot{M}_{BH}}{4\times10^{17}\mathrm{g}\text{ }\mathrm{s^{-1}}}\right)^\frac{17}{12},
    \label{agnRadioEmission}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\dot{M}_{\mathrm{BH}}$ is the accretion rate of the most massive black hole in each galaxy as catalogued by \textsc{caesar}. Since the bolometric radio power represents the monochromatic luminosity $P_{\mathrm{Rad}} \sim \nu P_\nu$ evaluated at 1.4 GHz, the spectral luminosity is obtained by normalising as $P_\nu(1.4\;\mathrm{GHz}) = P_{\mathrm{Rad}} / \nu_{\mathrm{ref}}$, where $\nu_{\mathrm{ref}} = 1.4\;\mathrm{GHz}$. To evaluate the AGN radio emission across a frequency range (as was done for star formation), we assume a power-law spectral energy distribution of the form

\begin{equation}
    P_\nu(\nu) = P_\nu(1.4\;\mathrm{GHz})\,\left(\frac{\nu}  {1.4\;\mathrm{GHz}}\right)^{-\alpha},
\end{equation}

\noindent adopting a spectral index of $\alpha = 0.7$ characteristic of radio loud AGN jets \parencite{thomasRadioGalaxyPopulation2021,angladaRadioJetsYoung2018}. The contribution of each galaxy to the observed specific intensity is then computed by evaluating $P_\nu$ at the rest frame frequency $\nu_{\mathrm{rest}} = \nu_{\mathrm{obs}}(1+z)$ and converting to observed flux density using \cref{eq:luminosityDistanceRelation} in frequency space. The AGN flux contributions from all galaxies in the lightcone are summed and divided by the survey solid angle in \cref{eq:solidAngle} to obtain the AGN component of the radio specific intensity as a function of observed frequency.

\subsection{Uncertainty Estimation}

Sources of statistical uncertainty such as the sampling variance arising from the finite volume of the light cone and the inherent cosmic variance of the underlying matter distribution have not yet been addressed. To quantify this, we apply a jackknife resampling method \parencite{bergerJackknifeVarianceEstimator2007}, dividing the light cone into $N = 16$ spatial quadrants defined by quantile bins in right ascension and declination. For each realisation $k = 0, \ldots, 15$, the $k^{\mathrm{th}}$ quadrant is excluded and the full EBL spectrum is recomputed from the ones remaining. The jackknife variance is then estimated as:

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_\mathrm{jk}^2 = \frac{N-1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \left(x_k - \bar{x}\right)^2,
\end{equation}

\noindent where $x_k$ is the EBL estimate from the $k^{\mathrm{th}}$ realisation and $\bar{x}$ is the mean over all realisations. This uncertainty is computed independently at each wavelength bin and propagated through to the final SED.

Three limitations of this approach are noted. First, the quadrant sub-samples are assumed spatially independent, which is not strictly satisfied on scales comparable to the largest structures in the simulation volume. Second, only statistical uncertainty is captured; systematic uncertainties arising from dust prescription or snapshot incompleteness are not propagated through the jackknife. Third, the number of realisations is limited by the simulation volume, a larger volume would permit finer spatial subdivision and a more robust variance estimate.

Overall, the predicted spectrum broadly reproduces the shape and amplitude of the observed EBL across the sampled wavelength range particularly the two expected peaks in the optical and far IR discussed earlier. The optical/NIR component is moderately under predicted, the far-IR peak is well-reproduced, and the radio is systematically over predicted. To isolate the contributions from distinct cosmic epochs, the EBL was computed separately in three redshift bins: $0 < z < 1$, $1 < z < 3$, and $3 < z < 7$, each constructed from an independent light cone with its own jackknife error estimate (\cref{fig:finalBinnedEBL}). Contributions from $3 < z < 7$ are of order $10^{-2}\,\mathrm{nW\,m^{-2}\,sr^{-1}}$, further supporting the decision to truncate the integration at $z = 7$.

Several physically motivated trends are apparent in the binned decomposition. The optical/NIR emission is dominated by the $0 < z < 1$ bin, as expected: the apparent brightness of stellar populations falls with distance, so the local universe contributes disproportionately to the integrated starlight background. The far IR component peaks in the $1 < z < 3$ bin, coinciding with the epoch of peak cosmic star formation activity. Photons travelling from these distances traverse greater dust column densities, with UV and optical radiation progressively reprocessed into the far IR, whose emission is then further redshifted to longer observed wavelengths. The radio emission is likewise most prominent at low redshift, where radio loud AGN are both abundant and subject to minimal cosmological dimming.

The individual redshift bin estimates carry noticeably larger jackknife uncertainties than the full redshift prediction. This is a consequence of cosmic variance: each narrow bin subtends a smaller comoving volume, making the jackknife sub-volumes more sensitive to the stochastic distribution of bright sources. When integrated over the full redshift range, these fluctuations partially cancel across the larger ensemble of sub-volumes, reducing the relative variance in the total EBL estimate. This behaviour is consistent with the result that cosmic variance scales inversely with the square root of the survey volume \parencite{pass}.

\subsection{The Optical/NIR Regime}

The \textsc{simba} simulations have demonstrated considerable success in reproducing key observational diagnostics relevant to the optical regime, including the stellar IMF, the galaxy stellar mass function, and broad star formation histories (SFHs) across cosmic time \parencite{daveSimbaCosmologicalSimulations2019}. Given this fidelity, the optical/NIR component of the predicted EBL was anticipated to be the most robustly constrained of the three regimes modelled here. While the overall spectral morphology and bin-by-bin behaviour are broadly consistent with expectation, the predicted amplitude systematically under predicts compared to the observational data.

Firstly, simulation snapshots were excluded from the integration due to missing \textsc{fsps} photometry in certain broadband filters, since the EBL was constructed by summing over all available snapshots, each excluded snapshot represents a gap in the cumulative light budget. This is likely to have a underestimating systematic effect on the total predicted flux, particularly if the missing snapshots correspond to epochs of elevated star formation activity.

Second, the reliability of the photometry used is contingent on the values computed and stored by \textsc{caesar} during the halo finding and galaxy cataloguing phase. The current pipeline adopts these pre-computed integrated galaxy properties rather than constructing a full SED per individual galaxy from first principles. This introduces a sensitivity to any systematic offsets or approximations present within \textsc{caesar}'s handling of stellar population properties. If the stored luminosities encode assumptions about mean stellar ages or metallicities that deviate from the true distribution within each galaxy, the integrated EBL will inherit those biases. A more robust approach would involve generating a bespoke SED for each simulated galaxy at each snapshot, applying stellar population synthesis (SPS) models conditioned on the individual metallicity and age distributions drawn directly from the SIMBA particle data. This would remove the dependence on \textsc{caesar}'s precomputed outputs entirely and would be the considered approach for future work.

Despite these limitations, the redshift evolution remains physically consistent. In particular, the behaviour observed at high redshift ($3 \lesssim z \lesssim 7$) exhibits fluctuations at short wavelengths, which can be attributed to the redshifting of intrinsic ultraviolet emission from young, star-forming galaxies. At these redshifts, the observed frame wavelengths probe rest frame emission blueward of the Lyman break at $1216(1+z) \text{angsrtrom symbol}$, where the contribution from massive, short-lived stars is significant. Additionally, the lower dust content in high-redshift galaxies reduces attenuation, allowing a larger fraction of ultraviolet radiation to escape and contribute to the observed SED. These effects naturally lead to enhanced variability and structure in the optical regime at high redshift.

\subsection{The Far Infrared Regime}

The far IR component of the predicted EBL is well reproduced overall, with the MBB prescription capturing the broad shape and amplitude of the observed far IR peak with reasonable accuracy.

An interesting feature of the far IR curve in \cref{fig:finalBinnedEBL} is its redshift dependence. The lowest redshift bins contribute more strongly at shorter wavelengths, while higher redshift bins dominate at longer wavelengths. This behaviour is expected because of two effects. Firstly, thermal dust emission is redshifted to larger $\lambda_\mathrm{obs}$ with increasing distance, and secondly dust star forming galaxies (DSFGs) dominate the early universe between $z=2\rightarrow6$ especially compared to the local universe, hence reprocessing a greater fraction of UV and optical radiation into the far IR \parencite{reuterCompleteRedshiftDistribution2020}

Although the single temperature MBB in \cref{eq:dustmbb} does reproduce the expected shape, representing each galaxy with a single effective temperature from \cref{eq:equivalentDustTemp} is a major simplification. Real galaxies host a distribution of grain temperatures set by different environments within the interstellar medium, including diffuse dust, molecular clouds, and star-forming regions, where this temperature mixing is not well represented by a single value but rather a distribution. The peak and Wein side of the far IR SED are adequately matched, but contributions from cooler dust in the Rayleigh-Jeans tail may be underestimated due to this \parencite{caseyFarinfraredSpectralEnergy2012}.

% L_FIR energy budget

The current model neglects several physical processes that can become important in the far IR regime. In particular, the interaction between dust and the CMB is not explicitly included. At high redshift and long wavelengths, CMB heating can modify the effective dust temperature and suppress the contrast of dust emission relative to the background. Omitting it is expected to produce a minor overestimation on the long wavelength tail, particularly in the highest redshift bins \parencite{desertInterstellarDustEmission2022}.
Additionally, the adopted continuum MBB model does not account for spectral line emission, which can contribute to the total infrared output in sub millimetre wavelength ranges. While these effects are not expected to dominate the overall shape of the FIR peak, they may contribute to discrepancies in the detailed spectral behaviour \parencite{smailPotentialInfluenceFarinfrared2011}.

Another limitation arises at shorter infrared wavelengths, where the model fails to reproduce the observed transition between the optical/NIR and far IR regimes. This region corresponds to the mid infrared, which is dominated by emission from polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and complex dust features. As this component is not included in the MBB model, the gap is entirely expected within the scope of this work. A more complete treatment of the infrared regime would require full radiative transfer modelling, for example using tools such as \textsc{powderday}, which can self consistently model dust absorption, scattering, and emission across a broad wavelength range. Or some more recent approaches using machine learning techniques, such as in \cite{dobbelsPredictingGlobalFarinfrared2020} to infer the full global far IR SED of the universe.

Jackknife resampling gives a dominant far IR peak at $\lambda_\mathrm{obs} = 171.53\,\mu$m with intensity $\nu I_\nu = 12.10 \pm 0.27 \; \mathrm{nW\,m^{-2}\,sr^{-1}}$ which is a relative uncertainty of $2.2\%$. This is substantially lower than the $7.2\%$ obtained for the optical peak, consistent with the far IR EBL being dominated by a smaller number of bright, dusty galaxies whose contribution converges rapidly with simulation volume. The dominant uncertainties in the far IR are therefore systematic rather than statistical.

\subsection{The Radio Regime}

The radio component while matching the general shape, does systematically over predict relative to the observational data across the modelled wavelength range.

One plausible interpretation of this is that current observational radio datasets used for the observational EBL are not very deep. Current radio surveys, even deep pointed observations, remain sensitivity limited and resolve only the brightest end of the source population \parencite{pass}. A substantial population of faint star-forming galaxies and low-luminosity AGN below current detection thresholds would, if included, raise the observed EBL and reduce the apparent discrepancy. This incompleteness effect is well documented at frequencies below a few GHz \parencite{pass}, and the curated observational data used here likely underestimates the true radio background for this reason. \section{Conclusions}

In this work, a prediction of the EBL has been constructed using the \textsc{simba} cosmological hydrodynamical simulation, spanning the wavelength range from the optical/NIR to the radio regime. By combining galaxy catalogues with lightcone techniques, a self-consistent estimate of the integrated cosmic spectral energy distribution (CSED) over the redshift range $0 \leq z \leq 7$ was obtained and compared to observational constraints.

The resulting EBL reproduces the expected bimodal structure of the cosmic energy distribution, with a primary peak arising from stellar emission in the optical/NIR and a secondary peak corresponding to dust reprocessing in the far infrared. The overall shape of the simulated spectrum is in good qualitative agreement with observations, indicating that the dominant physical processes governing photon production and energy redistribution in galaxies are broadly captured by the simulation framework.

Quantitatively, however, systematic discrepancies are present. The optical/NIR component is found to be under predicted, which is likely attributable to limitations in the use of precomputed photometry, missing simulation snapshots, and uncertainties in the modelling of dust attenuation. In contrast, the far infrared peak is well reproduced in both shape and normalisation, suggesting that the global dust energy budget in the simulation is reasonably consistent with observations, despite the simplified single temperature MBB approach. The radio component is over predicted relative to observational data, likely reflecting uncertainties in the empirical scaling relations used to model star formation and AGN contributions.

The redshift decomposed analysis demonstrates that the majority of the EBL originates from $z < 3$, consistent with the peak of cosmic star formation activity. Optical emission is dominated by low redshift galaxies, while the far infrared receives significant contributions from intermediate redshifts due to the combined effects of dust reprocessing and cosmological redshift. High redshift galaxies contribute primarily through redshifted ultraviolet emission, producing the structured tail observed in the optical regime.

Statistical uncertainties, estimated via jackknife resampling, are found to be small for the total EBL, with relative errors of order a few percent at the peak wavelengths. This indicates that the dominant sources of discrepancy between the simulated and observed EBL are systematic in nature, rather than arising from sampling variance. In particular, simplified treatments of stellar emission, dust physics, and radio processes represent the primary limitations of the current approach.

Several avenues for future work emerge from this analysis. A more complete treatment of the infrared regime would require the inclusion of mid infrared emission, particularly from PAHs, which are not captured in the present model. This could be achieved through full radiative transfer calculations using tools such as \textsc{powderday}, enabling a self-consistent modelling of dust absorption, scattering, and emission across all wavelengths. Additionally, extending the analysis to higher energy regimes, including X-ray and gamma-ray emission, would provide a more comprehensive picture of the total cosmic energy budget. Improvements to the modelling of radio emission, particularly through more physically motivated treatments of AGN jets and feedback, would also help resolve the observed discrepancies in the long-wavelength regime.

Overall, this work demonstrates that cosmological hydrodynamical simulations such as \textsc{simba} are capable of reproducing the broad features of the extragalactic background light across multiple wavelength regimes. At the same time, the remaining discrepancies highlight the sensitivity of the cosmic SED to the detailed modelling of baryonic processes, particularly those involving dust and feedback. As both simulations and observations continue to improve, comparisons of this kind will provide increasingly powerful constraints on models of galaxy formation and the evolution of the universe.
